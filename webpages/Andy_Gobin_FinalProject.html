Research question:

To what extent does race affect poverty in Districts 12 and 31?

Data, Activated!
Summer 2023

Introduction to the Districts:

District 12:

District 12 is defined as the East Tremont and Claremont Village areas of the Bronx. In 2021, there were an estimated 175,532 people in East Tremont, of which 0.5% identified as Asian, 29.9% identified as Black, 61.3% identified as Hispanic, and 3.6% identified as White. The poverty rate in the East Tremont area is around 35.8% as of 2021. These numbers come from the Furmancenter.org's data. Claremont village has a population of around 40,000 people with 61% Hispanic, 35% Black, and 2% White. 48.6% of the children in Claremont Village live below the federal poverty line. The polulation numbers come from the Harriet Tubman Charter School's data and the poverty numbers come from the Neighborhoodscout.com's data.

I am from District 12, I went to school in District 12, and that makes me a first hand witness of these numbers.

District 31:

District 31 encompasses the entirety of Staten Island in which there was an estimated 493,494 people in Staten Island in 2021, of which 11.4% identified as Asian, 8.8% identified as Black, 18.7% identified as Hispanic, and 57.5% identified as white. The poverty rate in Staten Island is 11.5% as of 2021. These numbers come from the Furmancenter.org's data.

Note: Each circle with a grade on them in the image represents a schools rating based on Niche.com's own grading scale, which is based on a system of "gathering data and reviews", "rigorous analysis", "user insights", and then "producing grades". Notice how District 31 schools have higher grades than school in District 12. In additon to District 31's schools holding overall higher ratings, District 31 also has more schools than than District 12 with 77 schools in total. While District 12 only has 51 schools. District 12 may have more high schools, but District 31 has more than double the elementary schools.

Key information to know:

    NYC DOE's definition of Poverty: NYC DOE "Poverty" counts are based on the number of students with families who have qualified for free or reduced price lunch, or are eligible for Human Resources Administration (HRA) benefits.

    How race data was collected: Enrollment counts are based on the October 31 Audited Register for the 2017-18 to 2019-20 school years. Counts are based on information given during enrollment.

    Method: I used correlation analysis in order to explain the relationship between two variables, being race and poverty. When using correlation analysis you find the coefficient of correlation which is a number between -1 and 1 which tells you whether the relation between the variables is strong, moderate, or weak. On top of that it tells yo if the relationship is a postitive or negative correlation if there is any correlation at all.

    Limitations of the data set:
    If the school has over 95% poverty it won’t give an exact number of students in poverty it will just say over 95%. Which is why most of the charts about the Bronx will only show a few plot points as it wouldn't let me plot any points where the poverty was over 95%.

    If parents enrolling their children consciously or subconsciously answer the surveys incorrectly that information will stay and affect the data set.

4.1.1 FY 2023 FSF
FSF allocates dollars to schools through five basic categories:
▪ FSF Foundation—a fixed sum of $225,000 for all schools
▪ FSF Grade weights, based on student grade levels
▪ FSF Needs weights, based on student needs
▪ Enhanced FSF weights for students in “portfolio” high schools
▪ CB related increases for staff funded with FSF

One of the specific reasons funding is weighted like this is because:
▪ Meet the needs of students who need the greatest support;

The part that does not make sense:

Bronx Little School Funding data & The Carteret School Funding data

The solution

Either District 12 is not being given enough money based on their needs or there is a mismangement of funds present.
The fix would be for the DOE to either adjust the formula used to calculate the Fair Student Funding to give enough funding to districts that need it the most, like District 12. Another possible thing the DOE can do is to make sure the fund allocated to school is being spent on things they actually need.
